Jōmon and Yayoi Roots

The Jōmon people (10,000 BCE) created Japan’s earliest culture. The Yayoi migrants (300 BCE) introduced rice farming and metal tools. The mix of their languages likely gave rise to Proto-Japanese.

Development of Writing

Chinese characters (Kanji) reached Japan in the 5th century CE. To fit native sounds, Japanese developed Hiragana (cursive) and Katakana (fragmented) scripts—still used today.

Historical Stages

  • Old Japanese (Before 800 CE): Written phonetically with Kanji.
  • Middle Japanese (800–1600): Rise of Hiragana/Katakana; Chinese influence grew.
  • Early Modern (1600–1868): Edo-period standardization and rich literature.
  • Modern Japanese (After 1868): Western words and education reforms shaped the language.

Features of Modern Japanese

  • Uses three scripts — Kanji, Hiragana, Katakana
  • Follows Subject–Object–Verb grammar
  • Employs Keigo (polite speech) to show respect
  • Uses pitch accent instead of stress

Cultural Reflection

Japanese mirrors values of respect, harmony (Wa), and tradition. Its structure promotes politeness and community spirit, while its poetry and art preserve cultural beauty.
